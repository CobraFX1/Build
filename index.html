Layout Analysis
Good 4-Quadrant Zoning System
Separating by function (progression, economy, community, magic) is a strong organizational pattern that real medieval towns used — keep it. Players intuitively learn which direction to walk.
Good Spawn Core + Highway Cross
The crossroads pattern is historically accurate for medieval town centers. The 30-block spawn circle acts as a roundabout plaza — excellent for player distribution.
Consider Add Secondary Paths
Real medieval villages had winding side alleys branching off main roads. Consider adding 3-block-wide cobblestone paths connecting nearby buildings within each quadrant to make exploration feel more organic.
Consider Fill Dead Space with Decor
The gaps between buildings can feel empty. Scatter small props: market carts, well pumps, flower beds, lamp posts, hay stacks, and hitching posts. These 1-3 block details make a massive difference.
Consider Terrain Elevation Changes
Flat spawn areas feel artificial. Consider raising the North Citadel quadrant 3-5 blocks higher (it's the "upper" progression area) and stepping down toward South Commons. This adds visual hierarchy and medieval castle hill vibes.
Tip Medieval Block Palette Rules
Stick to these material families: Stone (stone bricks, cobblestone, andesite) for foundations, Wood (spruce, dark oak, stripped logs) for upper floors, Terracotta/Wool for accent roofs. Avoid modern blocks like concrete or prismarine in the main village.
Tip Height Variation Strategy
Create a skyline: Mage Tower (28) > Top Island (20) > PvP Barracks (18) > Academy (16) > Tavern (14) > others. Never make two adjacent buildings the same height — staggered rooflines look far more authentic.
Tip Depth & Detail Layers
Every wall needs at least 2 layers of depth. Use stairs, slabs, and trapdoors to break up flat surfaces. Add window boxes (trapdoor + flower pot), timber framing (stripped logs), and support beam details (fences as pillars).
